Antennas Fundamentals

The antenna is a device for converting electromagnetic radiation into electrical currents. These antennas can also convert electrical currents to electromagnetic radiation as well. The simple dipole antenna can be modeled with the following.

\[I=I_0 \cos(\omega t\]

We can then model the function with the following:

\[e^{-i\omega t} = \cos(\omega t) - i\sin(\omega t)\] \[I_0 e^{-i\omega t} = I\]

We also know that the change in charge over time gives you the current

\[I = {dq \over dt}\]

If we let \(z\) be the position along the dipole then the following is true.

\[I = {dq \over dz}{dz \over dt}\] \[I = {dq \over dz}v\]

Given the original function derived in the previous notes, on larmor's radiation

\[{E_\bot = {q \dot{v} \sin\theta \over r c^2}}\]

We can describe the movement of this charge by subing in the following.

\[\int {dq \over dz} dz\] \[\int dq\] \[q = \int {dq \over dz} dz\] \[{E_\bot = \int {dq \over dz} dz{\dot{v} \sin\theta \over r c^2}}\]

We also know this is true.

\[\dot{v} = -i \omega v\] \[{E_\bot = \int {dq \over dz} dz{ -i \omega v \sin\theta \over r c^2}}\]

Since we know the following

\[I = {dq \over dz}v\]

We can sub it in for the integral as

\[{E_\bot = { -i \omega \sin\theta \over r c^2}}\int {dq \over dz} v dz\] \[{E_\bot = { -i \omega \sin\theta \over r c^2}}\int_{-l/2}^{l/2} I dz\]

We also know that the current depends on the position you measure on the rod. We also know that the current should reduce linearly and thus we have the current scaled by a factor of the ratio of position to the length of that dipole. That is why we have the \(1 - {\vert z \vert \over (l / 2)}\) scaling.

\[I(z) = I_0 e^{- i \omega t} \biggl[1 - {\vert z \vert \over (l / 2)} \biggr]\]

Now we take the integral of the following:

\[\int_{-l/2}^{+l/2} I dz = \int I_0 e^{- i \omega t} \biggl[1 - {\vert z \vert \over (l / 2)} \biggr] \] \[= I_0 e^{- i \omega t} \int_{-l/2}^{l/2} 1 dz - \int_{-l/2}^{l/2} {\vert z \vert \over (l / 2)} \] \[= I_0 e^{- i \omega t} [[z]\Big|_{-l/2}^{l/2} - [\int_{0}^{l/2} { z \over (l / 2)} + \int_{-l/2}^{0} {z \over (l / 2)} ] ]\] \[= I_0 e^{- i \omega t} [ l - [2/l { z^2 \over 2}\Big|_{0}^{l/2} + 2/l{z \over (2)}\Big|_{-l/2}^{0}]] \] \[= I_0 e^{- i \omega t} { l\over 2} \]

Thus if we subed in our original equation we get the following.

\[E_\bot = {-i \omega \sin\theta \over r c^2} {I_0 l \over 2} e^{-i \omega t}\]

We also know the following \(\omega = 2 \pi c / \lambda\)

\[E_\bot = {-i 2 \pi c \sin\theta \over \lambda r c^2 } {I_0 l \over 2} e^{-i \omega t}\]

We also derived flux previously with the following equation

\[\langle{S}\rangle = {c \over 4 \pi} \langle E_\bot^2 \rangle\]

We also know that the power output is the sum of all the flux over the entire surface and thus we have the following.

\[\langle P \rangle = \int\langle S \rangle dA = {c \over 4 \pi} \biggl({I_0 l \over \lambda} \, {\pi \over c} \biggr)^2 \biggl({1 \over 2}\biggr) \int_{\phi = 0}^{2\pi} \int_{\theta = 0}^\pi {\sin^2\theta \over r^2} r \sin\theta d \phi r d\theta\] \[{\langle P \rangle = {\pi^2 \over 3 c} \biggl({I_0 l \over \lambda}\biggr)^2 }\]

Therefore we get the following time-averaged power radiated by a short dipole

We also have Radiation Resistance with OMHS law we have the following

\[\langle P \rangle = \langle I^2 \rangle R\] \[\langle P \rangle = {I_0^2 R \over 2}\]

We also know the power

\[R = {2 \pi^2 \over 3 c} \biggl( {l \over \lambda} \biggr)^2\]

The Power Gain of a Transmitting Antenna

Power gain is defined by this function \(G(\theta,\,\phi)\) as the power transmitted per unit solid angle in direction \(\theta, \phi\) divided by the power transmitted per unit solid angle from an isotropic antenna driven by a transmitter supplying the same total power.

Since energy is conserved in a perfect scenario of a lossless antenna the gain averaged over all the area we get 1

\[\langle G \rangle \equiv {\int_{\rm sphere} G d \Omega \over \int_{\rm sphere } d \Omega} = {4 \pi \over 4 \pi}=1\] \[\int_{\rm sphere} G d \Omega = \int_{\rm sphere} 1 d \Omega = 4 \pi~\]

Different lossless antennas may radiate with different directional patterns, but they cannot alter the total amount of power radiated.

\[\Delta \Omega \approx {4 \pi \over G _{\rm max}}\]

The last part is finding the effective area of a receiving antenna. We know it as the following

\[A_e = {Power \over {power \over area }}\] \[A_e = {Power \over {flux}}\] \[A_e = {P_v \over S_{matched}}\] \[S_{\rm (matched)} = {S \over 2}\]

We need to try and find the power output of this antenna

\[P_\nu = A_{\rm e} S_{\rm (matched)} = A_{\rm e} {S \over 2} = \int_{4\pi} A_{\rm e}(\theta,\phi) {B_\nu \over 2} d\Omega\]

Using the Rayleigh-Jeans approximation

\[P_\nu = {2 k T \over 2 \lambda^2} \int_{4 \pi} A_{\rm e} d \Omega = k T\] \[\int_{4 \pi} A_{\rm e} d \Omega = \lambda^2\]

The average collecting area is defined by

\[\langle A_{\rm e} \rangle \equiv {\int_{4 \pi} A_{ \rm e} d \Omega \over \int_{4 \pi} d \Omega }\] \[{A_{\rm e}(\theta, \phi) = {\lambda^2 G(\theta, \phi) \over 4 \pi}}\]

We can also describe the antenna temprature as the following

\[{T_{\rm A} = {P_\nu \over k} = {A_{\rm e} S \over 2 k} }\rlap{\quad \rm {(3A10)}}\]

Beam Solid Angle

Beam angle is defined as the following

\[{\Omega_{\rm A} \equiv \int_{4 \pi} P_{\rm n} (\theta, \phi) d \Omega}\]

Where \(P_n\) is the normalized power by the maximum.

\[P_{\rm n} = {G(\theta, \phi) \over G_{\rm max}}\] \[\Omega_{\rm A} = {1 \over G_{\rm max}} \int_{4 \pi} G d \Omega = {4 \pi \over G_{\rm max}}\]

On the other hand we have the main beam solid angle which is the same thing except summed over the main beam region.

\[{\Omega_{\rm MB} \equiv \int_{\rm MB} P_{\rm n} (\theta, \phi) d \Omega}\]
