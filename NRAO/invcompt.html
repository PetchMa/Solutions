Inverse Compton Scattering

What is Compton Scattering?

Compton scattering is the idea that photons exist as particles and when striking free electrons, momentum must be conserved even if they are "massless". This is because of relativistic effects caused by the massless photon traveling at relativistic speeds to which in our frame the photon contains no mass. However, it can still collide because in its frame it "has" mass.

Compton scattering is simply elastic collision of photons at relativistic speeds. Consider the drawing:

With some basic understanding of momentum conservation and geometry we get the following in the x direction:

\[p_i= p_f \cos\theta + p \cos \phi\]

For the y direction we get

\[p_f \sin\theta = p\sin \phi\]

If we square both expressions and add them together we get the following

\[(p_i-p_f\cos\theta)^2 = p^2\cos^2\phi \] \[ p_f^2\sin^2 \theta = p^2 \sin^2\phi\]

We add the square expressions together

\[(p_i-p_f\cos\theta)^2 + p_f^2\sin^2 \theta = p^2\cos^2\phi +p^2 \sin^2\phi\]

We can simplify identity

\[p_i^2+p_f^2 -2p_ip_f\cos \theta = p^2\]

Then we can apply energy conservation. We know the energy of the incoming photon and the out going photon and the rest eneryg of the electron as \(m_ec^2\)

\[E_i +m_ec^2 = E_f +m_ec^2+E_k\]

We know that the kinentic energy of the photon electron is simply the energy change of the initial photon and the rebounded photon since the PHOTON is the one giving energy.

\[E_k = E_i-E_f\]

We can write the energy of the photon as the momentum

\[p={E \over c}\] \[E_k = c(p_i-p_f)\] \[E_i +m_ec^2 = E_f +m_ec^2+c(p_i-p_f)\]

Knowing this we can rewrite the relativistic equation in the following way.

\[E = m_ec^2 +E_k\]

Relativistic electron is all of it square is

\[E^2 = (m_ec^2)^2 +(c(p_i-0))^2\] \[E^2 = (m_ec^2)^2 +c^2 p^2\] \[(m_ec^2 +E_k)^2 =(m_ec^2)^2 +c^2 p^2\]

This simplifies to

\[{K^2 \over c^2} + 2E_Km_e = p^2\]

Now we can combine everything together.

\[p_i^2+p_f^2 -2p_ip_f\cos \theta ={K^2 \over c^2} + 2E_Km_e\]

Then we replace \(E_k = c(p_i-p_f)\) and we simplify we get the following

\[m_ec(p_i-p_f) = (1-\cos \theta) p_ip_f\]

We can then rewrite it all into this form

\[{1\over p_f} - {1 \over p_i} = {1\over m_e c}(1-\cos\theta)\]

If we multiplied everything by Plancks constant we arrive at the compton scattering

\[\lambda_f - \lambda_i {h\over m_e c}(1-\cos\theta)\]

What is INVERSE Compton scattering?

Inverse compton scattering is when an electron hits a ray and accelerates the ray of photons by some amount. The electron gives off energy to the photon, instead of photon giving energy to the electron!

Now with original compton scattering we can get the energy of the electron with this equation

\[E_1' = {E_0 \over 1+{E_0 \over m_ec^2}(1-\cos\phi)}\]

In order for there to be a substantial loss in energy as in describing the electron \(1+{E_0 \over m_ec^2}(1-\cos\phi)\) needs to be greater than 1 and thus \(E_0 >> m_ec^2\). This is for REGULAR compton scattering.

For inverse compton scattering needs to be relativistic frame since the electron is also moving, close to the speed of light. Thus we need to preform a lorentz transform on the Energy as follows

\[E_0' = \gamma E_0 (1-\frac{v}{c} \cos\theta_0)\]

Where \(1-\frac{v}{c} \cos\theta_0\) accounts for the doppler shifting of the energy.

Now we are in the relativistic frame we can say the following

\[E_1' = {E_0' \over 1+{E_0' \over m_ec^2}(1-\cos\phi')}\]

Now we can get back the non-relativistic frame.

\[E_1 = \gamma E_1'(1+\frac{v}{c} \cos\theta_1)\]

Now we can plug in values in terms of the different frames! Problem is, the algebra is horrendous. Thus we are gonna do a few limits so we have equations to solve for things when its useful.

The first limit is when \(E_0 << m_ec^2\) basically when it isnt very relativistic. Thus the bottom part is gonna disappear into just 1 and we get an approximate solution.

\[E_1' = {E_0 \over 1+(0)(1-\cos\phi)}\] \[E_1' = {E_0}\]

Plug things back we get =>

\[E_1 = \gamma^2 E_0 (1-\frac{v}{c} \cos\theta_0)(1+\frac{v}{c} \cos\theta_1)\]

The thing is the doppler shifts tend to equal to one, as you shift back and forth between red and blue shift of doppler effects

\[E_1 \approx \gamma^2 E_0 \]

Now if we were to consider if \(E_0 >> m_ec^2\) where it dominates the entire fraction then we would get an approximate

\[E_1 \approx m_ec^2\] \[E_1 ~ \gamma m_ec^2\]
