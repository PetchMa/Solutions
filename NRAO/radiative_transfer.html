Radiative Transfer

Radiative transfer is looking at radiation traveling through objects, rather than through free space. Radiation traveling through free space can be defined as, \(dI_v \over dS\) this is where S the in the direction through the medium to detector. \(\tau\) is the opposite direction of this transfer.

Absorbtion

Radiation can be absorbed by an element. The small change in probability of a photon being absorbed is \(dp_v\) agaisnt the \(dS\) and so the probability of an absorbtion on that frequency per slice of the volume, is a constant value.

\[k_v= {dp_v \over dS}\]

Why only take a thin slice of the absorber \(S\)? Because, the probability will no longer scale linearly with a thick volume. This simple macroscopic model doesn't depend on the microscopic physical processes by which photons are absorbed. It is like the ideal gas model, which describes the macroscopic properties of a gas without going into the microscopic details about how individual gas molecules behave.

What is the percent of intensity lost? That can be described here in the following. This is because the percent loss is the probability of absorbtion.

\[{dI_v \over I_v} = -dp_v\] \[{dI_v \over I_v} = -k_vdS\]

We can then integrate bothsides to get the following bounded by the entire solid.

\[\int_{s_{\rm in}}^{s_{\rm out}} {1 \over I_\nu} d I_\nu= -\int_{s_{\rm in}}^{s_{\rm out}} \kappa_\nu(s')ds' = \ln I_\nu \big|_{s_{\rm in}}^{s_{\rm out}}\] \[\ln[I_\nu(s_{\rm out})] - \ln[I_\nu(s_{\rm in})] = - \int_{s_{\rm in}}^{s_{\rm out}} \kappa_\nu (s') ds'\]

We can simplify the following

\[e^{\ln[I_\nu(s_{\rm out})] -\ln[I_\nu(s_{\rm in})]} = e^{- \int_{s_{\rm in}}^{s_{\rm out}} \kappa_\nu (s') ds'}\] \[{e^{\ln[I_\nu(s_{\rm out})]} \over e^{\ln[I_\nu(s_{\rm in})]}} = e^{- \int_{s_{\rm in}}^{s_{\rm out}} \kappa_\nu (s') ds'}\] \[I_\nu(s_{\rm out}) = I_\nu(s_{\rm in}) \cdot exp(- \int_{s_{\rm in}}^{s_{\rm out}} \kappa_\nu (s') ds')\] \[\bbox[border:3px blue solid,7pt]{\tau_\nu \equiv \int_{s_{\rm out}}^{s_{\rm in}} -\kappa_\nu(s') ds'}\]

We can say that \(\tau_\nu \) is the opacity of the solid because it represents the entire since we sum the infinitely small slices of the probability of photons that will be absorbed. Thus we can represent the following by plugging in the value we set from before. We described this as the negative path of S because we want the optical depth. How much the observer can see THROUGH the substance.

\[I_\nu(s_{\rm out}) = I_\nu( s_{\rm in}) e^{-\tau_\nu} \]

Emission

Emission happens when this radiate out light and photons based on its energy. Like a metal rod glowing from the heat. We can describe the emission process as the opposite of how described absorbtion, as the amount the intensity emitted per slice.

\[\bbox[border:3px blue solid,7pt]{\epsilon_\nu \equiv {dI_\nu \over ds}}\]

That is ignoring absorbtion however taking into acount both absorbtion and emission we get the following.

\[{d I_\nu \over ds} = 0\] \[{d I_\nu \over ds} = -{d I_\nu \over ds} +{d I_\nu \over ds}\] \[{-dI_v \over dS } = k_vI_v\] \[{\epsilon_\nu = {dI_\nu \over ds}}\] \[\bbox[border:3px blue solid,7pt]{{d I_\nu \over ds} = -\kappa_\nu I_\nu + \epsilon_\nu}\]

Thermodynamical Equilibrium

The law of thermodynamical equilibrium is the balance between the two isolated pools of energy. You cannot heat one and cool the other at the same time. Instead you bring the system to an equal amount of energy. If one part is heated and the other is cooled and the process continues indefintely, this creates energy from nothing.

Thus in thermodynamical equilibrium this must be true.

\[{d I_\nu \over ds} = 0 {\rm ~and~} I_\nu = B_\nu(T)\]

We sub it in

\[{d I_\nu \over ds} = 0 = -\kappa_\nu B_\nu (T) + \epsilon_\nu\] \[{{\epsilon_\nu (T) \over \kappa_\nu (T )} = B_\nu(T)}\]

We know the following equation from previous we can sub in values for it.

\[B_\nu \approx {2 k T \nu^2 \over c^2}~,\] \[I_\nu = {2 k T_{\rm b}(\nu) \nu^2 \over c^2}\]

Move the values around and we get the following

\[{T_{\rm b}(\nu) \equiv {I_\nu c^2 \over 2 k \nu^2}}\]

Lets take a real world example of light passing through the atmosphere. So we start off with the original equation.

\[{d I_\nu \over ds} = 0 = -\kappa_\nu B_\nu (T) + \epsilon_\nu\] \[{dI_\nu \over \kappa_\nu dS} = {-dI_\nu \over d_\tau}\] \[\epsilon_\nu = \kappa_\nu B_\nu(T_{\rm A})\] \[ {dI_\nu \over dS}= -\kappa_\nu B_\nu (T) + \kappa_\nu B_\nu(T_{\rm A})\] \[{dI_\nu \over dS} {1 \over \kappa_\nu}= - B_\nu (T) + B_\nu(T_{\rm A})\] \[{-dI_\nu \over d_\tau}= - B_\nu (T) + B_\nu(T_{\rm A})\]

We then multiply both sides by \(e^{-\tau}\) and integrate by parts

\[\frac{dI_\nu}{\kappa_\nu dS} = I_v\] \[V= I_v\] \[dV= {dI_v \over d\tau}\] \[U =e^{-\tau}\] \[dU =-e^{-\tau}\] \[\int e^{-\tau}{-dI_\nu \over d_\tau}= \int e^{-\tau}(- B_\nu (T) + B_\nu(T_{\rm A}))\] \[e^{-\tau} I_\nu \vert_0^{\tau_{\rm A}} - \int_0^{\tau_{\rm A}} -e^{-\tau} I_\nu\ d \tau = \int_0^{\tau_{\rm A}} I_\nu\ e^{-\tau} d\tau - B_\nu(T_{\rm A}) \int_0^{\tau_{\rm A}} e^{-\tau} d \tau\] \[I_\nu(\tau = 0) = ( 1 - e^{-\tau_{\rm A}}) B_\nu(T_{\rm A})\]

However, we need a function for \(\tau_A\) but it is a function of the zenth which is normal to the ground. Thus we have the following.

\[\tau_{\rm A} = \tau_{\rm Z}\, {\rm sec}\, z\] \[I_\nu = [1 - \exp(-\tau_{\rm Z}\, {\rm sec} \, z)]{2 k T_{\rm A} \nu^2 \over c^2}\] \[T_{\rm b} = {I_\nu c^2 \over 2 k \nu^2} = T_{\rm A} \biggl[1 - \exp (-\tau_{\rm Z}\,{\rm sec}\,z)\biggr]\]

Emission, Absorption, and Reflection From an Opaque Body

Assuming a completely opaque body. The absorption coefficient a is the probability that an incident photon of frequency will be absorbed. The reflection coefficient r is the probability that an incident photon of frequency will be reflected.

In equilibrium, the spectral power absorbed per unit area, Pa, must equal the power emitted per unit area.

\[P_{\rm a} = a_\nu \int B_\nu \cos \theta d \Omega \] \[P_{\rm a} = a_\nu \int_{\phi = 0}^{2\pi} \int_{\theta=0}^{\pi/2} B_\nu \cos\theta \sin\theta d \theta d \phi\] \[P_{\rm a} = a_\nu \int_{\theta=0}^{\pi/2}\int_{\phi = 0}^{2\pi} B_\nu \cos\theta \sin\theta d \phi d \theta \] \[P_{\rm a} = a_\nu 2 \pi B_\nu \int_0^{\pi/2} \cos \theta \sin \theta d \theta\] \[P_{\rm a} = a_\nu \times \pi B_\nu\]

We also have the spectral emissivity. Which is given by the following property.

\[\bbox[border:3px blue solid,7pt]{e_\nu = a_\nu = 1 - r_\nu}\] \[T_{\rm b}(\nu) = a_\nu T = (1 - r_\nu) T\]

Thus if something reflects all the light

\[T_{\rm b}(\nu) = 0\]
