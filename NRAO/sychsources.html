Synchrotron Sources

We want to find the total energy density of a source of scyhlotron emission And so in order to find \(U_e\) energy density and \(U_{\rm B} = B^2 / (8 \pi)\) We Need to take the integral of the energy disribution of the electrons.

\[N(E) \approx K E^{-\delta}\]

where \(N(E)dE\) is the number of electrons per unit volume with energies \(E\) to \(E+dE\)

\[\N(E) \approx K E^{-\delta}]

Where \(N(E)\) was the electron energy distribution that said how much electrons given said energy. Then the entire energy density is given

\[U_{\rm e} = \int_{E_{\rm min}}^{E_{\rm max}} E N(E)\, d E\]

We know that the luminosity is the following:

\[L = \int_{\nu_{\rm min}}^{\nu_{\rm max}} L_\nu d \nu\]

We can rewrite luminosity as a multiplication of power and distribution of enery for number of electrons

\[L = \int_{E_{\rm min}}^{E_{\rm max}} P N(E)\, dE\] \[L = \int_{E_{\rm min}}^{E_{\rm max}} {dE \over dt} N(E)\, dE\]

Thus the energy density per luminosity we get that

\[{U_{\rm e} \over L} \propto { \int_{E_{\rm min}}^{E_{\rm max}} E N(E)\, dE \over -\int_{E_{\rm min}}^{E_{\rm max}} (dE/dt) N(E)\, dE}\]

Substituting \(N(E)=KE^{-\sigma}\) and the synchrotron power emitted per electron \({âˆ’dE\over dt} \propto B^2E^2\) gives

\[{U_{\rm e} \over L} \propto { K \int_{E_{\rm min}}^{E_{\rm max}} E^{1 - \delta} \, dE \over K B^2 \int_{E_{\rm min}}^{E_{\rm max}} E^{2-\delta} \, dE}\] \[{U_{\rm e} \over L} \propto {E^{2 - \delta} \vert_{E_{\min}}^{E_{\rm max}} \over B^2 E^{3 - \delta} \vert_{E_{\rm min}}^{E_{\rm max}} }\]

Since electrons with energy E emit most of the radiation seen at frequency \(\nu \propto E^2 B\), the electron energy needed to produce radiation at frequency scales as

\[E \propto B^{-1/2}\] \[{U_{\rm e} \over L } \propto { (B^{-1/2})^{2 - \delta} \over B^2 (B^{-1/2})^{3 - \delta} } = {B^{-1 + \delta/2} \over B^2 B^{-3/2 + \delta/2}} = B^{-3/2}\]

The "invisible" cosmic-ray protons and heavier ions emit negligible synchrotron power but they still contribute to the total cosmic-ray particle energy. If we call the ion/electron energy ratio , then the total energy density in cosmic rays is

\[(1 + \eta) U_{\rm e}\] \[{U = (1 + \eta) U_{\rm e} + U_{\rm B}}\]

We can also now find the min of the energy.

\[{d U \over d B} = {d [(1 + \eta) U_{\rm e} + U_{\rm B}] \over d B} = 0\]

We need to find the derivative of each of the energy density by doing the following trick.

\[{d U_{\rm e} \over d B} \cdot U_{\rm e}^{-1} = -\biggl( {3 \over 2} \biggr) B^{-5/2} B^{3 / 2} = -{3 \over 2 B}\]

Multiply it over and we get the following

\[{dU_{\rm e} \over d B} = - {3 U_{\rm e} \over 2 B}\]

Same thing as what we have done before with the energy density of the electric field.

\[{d U_{\rm B} \over d B} \cdot U_{\rm B}^{-1} = { 2 B \over B^2 } = {2 \over B}\] \[{dU_{\rm B} \over d B} = { 2 U_{\rm B} \over B}\]

Thus if we put everything together and tried to solve for the minimum we get the following

\[{d [(1 + \eta) U_{\rm e}] \over d B} + {d U_{\rm B} \over d B} = 0 = -{ 3 (1 + \eta) U_{\rm e} \over 2 B} + {2 U_{\rm B} \over B}\]

\[\tau \equiv {E_{\rm e} \over L}\]

The Eddington Limit

The Eddington Limit is the limit between the radiation pressure and the gravitational pressures. The outward radiation pressure cannot exceed gravity.

\[{L_{\rm E} \over 4 \pi r^2 c} \sigma_{\rm T} = {GM (m_{\rm p} + m_{\rm e}) \over r^2} \approx {GM m_{\rm p} \over r^2}\]

Where the \(m_p, m_e\) are all the proton and electron masses which can be simply approximated.

\[L_{\rm E} \approx { 4 \pi G M m_{\rm p} c \over \sigma_{\rm T}}\]
