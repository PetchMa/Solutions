Matrix and Vector Transformation

Problem 1

Prove: that \(T(\begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \\ \end{bmatrix}) = \begin{bmatrix} a+b \\ b-a \\ \end{bmatrix}\) is a linear transformation.

Direct Proof

We know that the definition of a linear transformation as the following.

\[1. \quad T(c\vec{v} )=cT(\vec{v})\] \[2. \quad T(\vec{u} +\vec{v} )=T(\vec{u})+T(\vec{v})\]

We can show the first case easily.

\[T(c\begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \\ \end{bmatrix}) = T(\begin{bmatrix} ca \\ cb \\ \end{bmatrix})\] \[T(c\begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \\ \end{bmatrix}) = \begin{bmatrix} ca+cb \\ cb-ca \\ \end{bmatrix}\] \[T(c\begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \\ \end{bmatrix}) = \begin{bmatrix} c(a+b) \\ c(b-a) \\ \end{bmatrix}\] \[T(c\begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \\ \end{bmatrix}) = c\begin{bmatrix} a+b \\ b-a \\ \end{bmatrix}\] \[T(c\begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \\ \end{bmatrix}) = cT(\begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \\ \end{bmatrix})\]

We can then prove the second condition. As well.

\[T(\begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \\ \end{bmatrix}+\begin{bmatrix} c \\ d \\ \end{bmatrix} ) = T(\begin{bmatrix} a+c \\ b+d \\ \end{bmatrix})\] \[T(\begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \\ \end{bmatrix}+\begin{bmatrix} c \\ d \\ \end{bmatrix} ) = \begin{bmatrix} (a+c)+(b+d) \\ (b+d)-(a+c) \\ \end{bmatrix}\] \[T(\begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \\ \end{bmatrix}+\begin{bmatrix} c \\ d \\ \end{bmatrix} ) = \begin{bmatrix} (a+b)+(c+d) \\ (b-a)+ (d+c) \\ \end{bmatrix}\] \[T(\begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \\ \end{bmatrix}+\begin{bmatrix} c \\ d \\ \end{bmatrix} ) = \begin{bmatrix} a+b \\ b-a \\ \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} c+d \\ d+c\\ \end{bmatrix}\] \[T(\begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \\ \end{bmatrix}+\begin{bmatrix} c \\ d \\ \end{bmatrix} ) = T(\begin{bmatrix} a \\ b\\ \end{bmatrix}) + T(\begin{bmatrix} c \\ d\\ \end{bmatrix})\]

Therefore the transformation is linear.

Problem 2

Fix v be an element in a vector space \(V\) over a field \(\mathbb{F}\) and define:

\[S = \{w \in V: \langle v,w \rangle = 0\}\]

Prove: the following... note that: \(\langle v, w \rangle\) is the dot product.

\(0 \in S\)

This is easy to prove. We know that for any \(\langle v, 0\rangle = 0\) Thus \(0 \in S\)

\(\forall \alpha \in F, \forall s \in S, \alpha · s \in S\)

We test to see if \(a \cdot s \in S\) if we plug it in... \(\langle v, a \cdot s \rangle\), thus \( a \cdot \langle v, s \rangle\) Since \(s \in S\) we know that the dot product with \(v\) must have equalled to \(0\) based on the definition. So we get \( a \cdot 0 = 0 \) which proves that \(a \cdot s \in S\)

\(∀s, t ∈ S, s + t ∈ S\)

We can plug this bitch in to the original definition. \(\langle v, (s+t)\rangle\). Dot product is distributive, \(\langle v, s\rangle + \langle v, t\rangle = 0 + 0 = 0 \) Thus it belongs to the set.
