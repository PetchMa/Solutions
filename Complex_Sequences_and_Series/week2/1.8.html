Problem 1.8

Suppose that \(z \neq âˆ’1\) is a complex number of unit magnitude: \(|z| = 1\). Show that \(w =\frac{ 1+z}{|1+z|}\) is a square root of z.

Solution

If it is true that \(w = \sqrt{z}\) \( \Rightarrow w^2 = z\).

Let \(y = w^2 - z\). We can also let \(k = 1+z\), then \(w =\frac{ k}{|k|}\). If \(y=0 \Rightarrow w =\sqrt{z} \)

Since we know that \(\frac{1}{k} = \frac{\bar{k}}{|k^2|}\), then \(\frac{\bar{k}k}{|k|^2} = 1\). Thus we can rewrite the following \(z =z \cdot \frac{\bar{k}k}{|k^2|} \)

\[y = w^2 - z\] \[y = (\frac{ 1+z}{|1+z|})^2 - z \cdot \frac{\bar{k}k}{|k^2|}\] \[y = \frac{ (1+2z+z^2)}{|1+z|^2} - z \cdot \frac{\bar{k}k}{|(z+1)^2|}\] \[y = \frac{ (1+2z+z^2) - z \cdot\bar{k}k }{|1+z|^2} \]

We know that the only imaginary part of \(k\) is contained in the component within \(z\). Since the complex conjugate contains the negative complex part of its non conjugate form. \(z =a+bi\), then \(1+z = 1+a+bi\) and the complex conjugate would then be \((1+a)-bi = 1+(a-bi)=1+\bar{z}\)

\[\bar{k} = 1+\bar{z}\] \[y = \frac{ (1+2z+z^2) - z \cdot(1+\bar{z})(1+z) }{|1+z|^2} \] \[y = \frac{ (1+2z+z^2) - z (1+\bar{z}+z+z\bar{z}) }{|1+z|^2} \] \[y = \frac{ (1+2z+z^2) - (z+\bar{z}z+z^2+z\cdot z \cdot\bar{z}) }{|1+z|^2} \]

We know that \(z\cdot \bar{z} = |z|^2\). We know that \(|z|=1\) then we get that \(z \cdot\bar{z} = 1\)

\[y = \frac{ (1+2z+z^2) - (z+(1)+z^2+z\cdot (1)) }{|1+z|^2} \] \[y = \frac{ (1+2z+z^2) - (z+1+z^2+z) }{|1+z|^2} \] \[y = \frac{ (1+2z+z^2) - (1+2z+z^2) }{|1+z|^2} \] \[y = \frac{ 0 }{|1+z|^2} \] \[y = 0\]

Since \(y=0\), \(\forall z \in \mathbb{C}\), \(\therefore \frac{ 1+z}{|1+z|} = \sqrt{z}\)
