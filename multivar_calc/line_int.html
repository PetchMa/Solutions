Line Integrals

Intro: Line integrals are integrals of a vector line over a certain path. Imagine a line integral as draping a curtain over a line and finding the area under that line.

The standard form of a line integral is the taking infinitesimally small lengths along a line and calculating the area under it. This is why for the line integral follows the this form.

\[\int_{c} f(x,y,z)ds = \int_{a}^{b} f(x(t),y(t),z(t)) \sqrt{(\frac{dx}{dt})^2+(\frac{dy}{dt})^2+(\frac{dz}{dt})^2} dt \]

For example this is the approximation for the line integral.

\[ \lim_{n \to \inf} \sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i, y_i,z_i)\Delta S = \int_{c} f(x,y,z)ds \]

Line integrals might be hard to compute because finding the parametric equations for the path components might be possible all the time. However, there're a few techniques around this.

Problem: If C is the line segment from(0,0,0) to (1,2,3) find the line integral of

\[\int_{c} xe^{yz}ds\]

This is simple because over the "surface" we draw a straight path. \(p =(0,0,0)\) and \(q=(1,2,3)\)

\[\vec{r}(t) = \langle p_x.p_y, p_z\rangle + t\langle q_x - p_x, q_y - p_y, q_z - p_z\rangle\] \[\vec{r}(t) = \langle 0,0,0\rangle + t\langle 1, 2, 3\rangle\] \[x(t)=t\] \[y(t)=2t\] \[z(t)=3t\]

We can then subsitute the following into the integral. We also know that the bounds of the inegral is determined by the end paths. Thus we can solve for values of t that give the two points.

\[t \in [0,1]\] \[\int_{0}^{1} te^{2t*3t}ds\] \[\int_{0}^{1} te^{2t*3t}\sqrt{(\frac{dx}{dt})^2+(\frac{dy}{dt})^2+(\frac{dz}{dt})^2} dt\] \[\int_{0}^{1} te^{2t*3t}\sqrt{(1)^2+(2)^2+(3)^2} dt\] \[\int_{0}^{1} te^{6t^2}\sqrt{14} dt\]

The we can U substitute for the values of \(6t^2\)

\[\frac{\sqrt{14}}{12}\int_{0}^{1} e^{u} du\] \[\frac{\sqrt{14}}{12}[e^{6t^2}]\Big|_{0}^{1}\] \[\frac{\sqrt{14}}{12}(e^{6}-1)\]
