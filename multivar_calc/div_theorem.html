Divergence Theorem

Intro: The divergence theorem is a way of computing the flux over a given boundary of a vector field. In 2d We have the line integral and we seek to measure the amount of flux across a boundary of curve c is equal to the divergence inside a 2D region.

\[\int_{c} \vec{F} \cdot \widehat{n} ds = \int \int_{D} div F dA\]

For 3D the divergence theorem calculates the flux across the boundary surface S by taking the sum of divergence inside a solide E.

\[\int_{S} \vec{F} \cdot \widehat{n} ds = \int \int \int_{E} div F dA\]

Mini Proof: For divergence theorem in 2-D

\[\int_{c} F \cdot \widehat{n} ds\]

We want to find the sum of how much the vector field is diverging from the contour of c. We do so by suming up each individual vector coming from the contour and dot it with the normal of the contour. This will show how much flux is experienced on a given boundary. This is useful as it can help determine, the velocity of a particle moving directly out of a boundary. Thus we need to dot the normal (directly outwards vector) with the actual vector to see how much that particle is moving directly outwards. Here is what we know...

\[\widehat{n} = dyi-dxj\] \[\vec{F} = \langle P,Q\rangle \] \[int_{c} F \cdot \widehat{n} ds\] \[int_{c} \langle P,Q\rangle \cdot \langle dy,-dx \rangle ds\] \[int_{c} \langle P,Q\rangle \cdot \langle dy,-dx \rangle ds\] \[int_{c} \langle Pdy - Qdx ds\]

We can use the Green's theorem to help solve the rest of the equation.

\[int_{c} \langle (- Q)dx + Pdy ds = \int \int{D} \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial -Q}{\partial y} dA\] \[ = \int \int_{D} \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} dA\] \[ = \int \int_{D} \nabla \cdot \langle P, Q \rangle dA\] \[ = \int \int_{D} \nabla \cdot F dA\]

Problem: What is the flux of the vector field where S is the surface of the solid bounded by the paraboloid \(z = 1 − x^2 − y^2\) and the xy-plane?

\[F(x,y,z) = x^3i+2xz^2j+3y^2zk\] \[F(x,y,z) = \langle x^3, 2xz^2,3y^2z \rangle \] \[\nabla \cdot F(x,y,z) = 3x^2+0+3y^2 \] \[\nabla \cdot F(x,y,z) = 3x^2+0+3y^2 \] \[int_{c} F \cdot \widehat{n} ds = \int \int \int_{D} \nabla \cdot F dA\] \[int_{c} F \cdot \widehat{n} ds = \int \int \int_{D} 3x^2+3y^2 dA\]

Notice if we switch to cylindrical coordinates this would be super easy to solve the integral.

\[ = \int_{0}^{2\pi} \int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{1-r^2} 3r^2 dzdrd\theta\] \[ = \int_{0}^{2\pi} \int_{0}^{1} 3r^3-3r^5 drd\theta\] \[ = \int_{0}^{2\pi} \frac{3}{4}r^4-\frac{1}{2}r^6 \Big|_{0}^{1} d\theta\] \[ = \frac{1}{4}\theta\Big|_{2\pi}{0}\] \[\int_{c} F \cdot \widehat{n} ds = \frac{\pi}{2}\]
