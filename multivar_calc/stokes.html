Stokes Theorem

Intro: Stokes theorem is an awesome way to relate a line integral to an surface integral in higher dimensions. You can think of this as the generalized form of Greens theorem! Before we took the scalar curl of the the path in 2D, now we can take the curl of the entire vector field F and preform an surface integral over that force field.

\[\oint_{c} \vec{F} dr = \int \int_{S} \nabla \times F \cdot \widehat{n} dA\]

Problem: Given a counter clockwise path of the the triangle \((1,0,0)\), \((0,1,0)\) and \((0,0,1)\) find the path integral of \(F(x,y,z)= (x^2+y)i+(y+z^2)j+(z+x^2)k\)

\[\oint_{c} \vec{F} dr = \int \int_{S} \nabla \times F \cdot \widehat{n} dA\] \[\oint_{c} (x^2+y)i+(y+z^2)j+(z+x^2)k) dr = \int \int_{S} \nabla \times (x^2+y)i+(y+z^2)j+(z+x^2)k) \cdot \widehat{n} dA\] \[\nabla \times (x^2+y)i+(y+z^2)j+(z+x^2)k) = (0-2z)i+(0-2x)j+(-1)k \] \[\nabla \times (x^2+y)i+(y+z^2)j+(z+x^2)k) = (-2z)i-(2x)j-k \] \[\nabla \times F= \langle P, Q, R \rangle \] \[\nabla \times F= \langle-2z, -2x, -1 \rangle \]

We then need a way to describe the surface at which we are trying to solve for, because this is relating a line integral to a surface integral. The region bounded by this plane is simply \(z+y+x =1 \) since this plane intersects both at the triangle described above.

\[z+y+x =1\] \[\oint_{c} \vec{F} dr = \int \int_{S} (P\frac{dz}{dx} +Q\frac{dz}{dy} +R\frac{dz}{dz} )dA\]

This may look weird as to why we are multiplying each by the derivative of G. Here is why, remember that the surface integral is defined as the following:

\[S= \int \int_{\Sigma} f(x(s,t),y(s,t),z(s,t))\cdot |\frac{\partial \vec{r}}{\partial s} \times \frac{\partial \vec{r}}{\partial t}|dsdt\]

Since we know that the scalar definition of a plane is infact \(\vec{t} \times \vec{s}\)

\[||\nabla f|| = |\frac{\partial \vec{r}}{\partial s} \times \frac{\partial \vec{r}}{\partial t}|\] \[ \widehat{n} = \frac{\nabla f}{||\nabla f||}\]

Therefore we have the following:

\[\oint_{c} \vec{F} dr = \int \int_{S} \langle P,Q,R \rangle \cdot \nabla f dA\]

Now if we were to rearrange the equation of the plane, we arrive at the following:

\[f(x,y,z) = x+y+z-1\] \[\nabla f(x,y,z) = \langle \frac{\partial f}{\partial x }, \frac{\partial f}{\partial y }, \frac{\partial f}{\partial z }\rangle\] \[\nabla f(x,y,z) = i+j+k\] \[\oint_{c} \vec{F} dr = \int \int_{S} \langle P, Q, R \rangle \cdot \langle \frac{\partial f}{\partial x }, \frac{\partial f}{\partial y }, \frac{\partial f}{\partial z }\rangle\ dA\] \[\oint_{c} \vec{F} dr = \int \int_{S} \langle -2z,-2x,-1 \rangle \cdot \langle 1,1,1\rangle\ dA\] \[\oint_{c} \vec{F} dr = \int \int_{S} (-2z-2x-1) dA\]

We know that the surface we are looking at is only bounded by xy and the bounds correspond to the xy projection. We know the triangle is described by the following:

\[x \in [0,1]\] \[y \in [0,1-x]\] \[\oint_{c} \vec{F} dr = \int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{1-x} (-2z-2x-1) dydx\]

We can replace z for the plane equation

\[\oint_{c} \vec{F} dr = \int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{1-x} (-2+2x+2y-2x-1) dydx\] \[\oint_{c} \vec{F} dr = \int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{1-x} (2y-3) dydx\] \[\oint_{c} \vec{F} dr = \int_{0}^{1} [y^2-3]\Big|_{0}^{1-x} dx\] \[\oint_{c} \vec{F} dr = \int_{0}^{1} [y^2-3y]\Big|_{0}^{1-x} dx\] \[\oint_{c} \vec{F} dr = \int_{0}^{1}x^2+x-2 dx\] \[\oint_{c} \vec{F} dr = [\frac{1/3}x^3+\frac{1}{2}x^2-2x]Big|_{0}^{1} dx\] \[\oint_{c} \vec{F} dr = [\frac{1/3}x^3+\frac{1}{2}x^2-2x]Big|_{0}^{1} dx\] \[\oint_{c} \vec{F} dr = \frac{-7}{6}\]
