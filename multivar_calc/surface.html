Surface Integrals

Intro: The surface integral is finding the area given a surface defined by \(\vec{r}(s,t)=\langle p_x,p_y,p_z\rangle + t\vec{m}+ s\vec{v}\). However each value of that surface is defined by \(f(x,y,z)\). Given the region defined by the surface \(\vec{r}(s,t)\) we can find the area of a very small parallelogram by taking the magnitude of the cross product of a very small change in the s and t values. This allows us to represent the surface integral as the following.

\[S= \int \int_{\Sigma} d \delta\] \[S= \int \int_{\Sigma} |\frac{\partial \vec{r}}{\partial s}ds \times \frac{\partial \vec{r}}{\partial t}dt|\] \[S= \int \int_{\Sigma} |\frac{\partial \vec{r}}{\partial s} \times \frac{\partial \vec{r}}{\partial t}|dsdt\] \[S= \int \int_{\Sigma} f(x,y,z)|\frac{\partial \vec{r}}{\partial s} \times \frac{\partial \vec{r}}{\partial t}|dsdt\] \[S= \int \int_{\Sigma} f(x(s,t),y(s,t),z(s,t))|\frac{\partial \vec{r}}{\partial s} \times \frac{\partial \vec{r}}{\partial t}|dsdt\]

Sometimes to simplify this notation we can choose to rewrite it as the following if we are given the plane in scalar form. We don't need to preform the cross product because the scalar form is the cross product of the s and t values!

\[S= \int \int_{\Sigma} f(x,y,g(x,y))\sqrt{1+(\frac{dz}{dx})^2+(\frac{dz}{dy})^2}dA\]

Problem: Evaluate the surface integral of: \(\int \int_{s}(x^2z+y^2z) ds\) where s is the hemisphere \(x^2+y^2+z^2=4\) and \(z \geq 0\)

\[S = \int \int_{s}(x^2z+y^2z) ds\] \[S= \int \int_{\Sigma} f(x,y,g(x,y))\sqrt{1+(\frac{dz}{dx})^2+(\frac{dz}{dy})^2}dA\]

We can find the values of \(\frac{dz}{dx}\) and \(\frac{dz}{dy}\) through implicit differentiation

\[x^2+y^2+z^2=4\] \[z^2=4-x^2-y^2\] \[2z\frac{dz}{dx}=-2x\] \[\frac{dz}{dx}=\frac{-x}{z}\] \[2z\frac{dz}{dy}=-2y\] \[\frac{dz}{dy}=\frac{-y}{z}\] \[S= \int \int_{\Sigma} f(x,y,g(x,y))\sqrt{1+(\frac{-x}{z})^2+(\frac{-y}{z})^2}dA\] \[S= \int \int_{\Sigma} (x^2z+y^2z)\sqrt{\frac{z^2+x^2+y^2}{z^2}}dA\] \[S= \int \int_{\Sigma} (x^2+y^2)\sqrt{z^2+x^2+y^2}dA\]

We can then sub in the value of \(z^2+x^2+y^2=4\)

\[S= 2\int \int_{\Sigma} (x^2+y^2)dA\]

We can make this easier to solve if we made this into polar coordinates. Like so.

\[S= 2\int \int_{\Sigma} (r^2)rdrd\theta\] \[S= 2\int \int_{} (r^2)rdrd\theta\]

Since there is no bounds for the quadrants of x and y we can simply say \(\theta \in [0, 2\pi]\) we also know the radius is 2/p> \[S= 2\int_{0}^{2\pi} \int_{0}^2 (r^3)drd\theta\]

Then we can take this and evaluate it

\[S= 2\int_{0}^{2\pi} \frac{1}{4}r^4\Big|_{0}^{2}d\theta\] \[S= 16\pi\]
