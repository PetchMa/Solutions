Double Integrals + Polar Coordinates

Intro: Double integrals is simply the area under the curve \(f(x,y)\) or the anti-derivative for the that multivariate function. Regularly we are given an integral over f such that its bounded by R

\[\int \int_{R}f(x,y) dA \] \[R = \{(x,y)| 0\leq x\leq 1, -3 \leq y\leq 3\} \]

Problem: Evaluate the double integral.


\[\int \int_{R} \frac{xy^2}{x^2+1} dA \] \[R = \{(x,y)| 0\leq x\leq 1, -3 \leq y\leq 3\} \]

We need to rewrite the original integral in terms of the bounds as an iterated integral.

\[\int_{0}^{1} \int_{-3}^{3} \frac{xy^2}{x^2+1} dydx \] \[R = \{(x,y)| 0\leq x\leq 1, -3 \leq y\leq 3\} \]

After rewriting the integral as an iterated integral we can integral in respect to y and x.

\[=\int_{0}^{1} \int_{-3}^{3} \frac{xy^2}{x^2+1} dydx \] \[=\int_{0}^{1} [\frac{xy^3}{3(x^2+1)}]\Big|_{-3}^{3} dx \] \[=\int_{0}^{1} [\frac{x(3)^3}{3(x^2+1)}-\frac{x(-3)^3}{3(x^2+1)} ] dx \] \[=\int_{0}^{1} [\frac{27x}{3(x^2+1)}+\frac{+27x)^3}{3(x^2+1)} ] dx \] \[=\int_{0}^{1} [\frac{18x}{(x^2+1)} ] dx \]

U subsitution technique for \(u = x^2+1\)

\[=\frac{1}{2}\int_{0}^{1} \frac{1}{u} du \] \[=\frac{1}{2}[18\ln|x^2+1|]\Big|_{0}^{1} \] \[=9\ln|2| \]

Polar Coordinates

Intro: Polar cooredinates are another system to represent functions that make certain types of problems easier to solve. Polar coordinates are written in the form \(r, \theta\) where r represents the radius and \(\theta\) represents the angle between the positve x axis and the line formed from point to the center.

Conversion formulas between cartesian coordinates to polar are the following:

\[x= r \cos (\theta)\] \[y= r \sin (\theta)\] \[x^2+y^2=r^2\]

Some problems are just easier to solve with polar coordinates. For example:

\[\int \int_{D} e^{x^2+y^2} dA \] \[D \in \{ y= \pm \sqrt{25-x^2}\} \]

The bounds are quite difficult for us to figure out in normally in cartesian however it is very easy in polar coordinates

\[x^2+y^2=r^2\] \[\int \int_{D} e^{r^2} drd\theta \]

Since \(x= r \cos (\theta)\) and \(y= r \sin (\theta)\) we can sub into the bounds

\[y=\pm \sqrt{25-x^2}\] \[r\sin(\theta)=\pm \sqrt{25-r^2\cos^2(\theta)}\] \[r^2\sin^2(\theta)=25-r^2\cos^2(\theta)\] \[r^2\sin^2(\theta)+r^2\cos^2(\theta)=25\] \[r^2(\sin^2(\theta)+\cos^2(\theta))=25\] \[r=\pm5\]

Since r represents the magnitude, r=5. Remember: to multiply the result by \(r dr\) as it accounts for the width of the area of the wedge.

\[\int_{0}^{2\pi} \int_{0}^{5} re^{r^2} drd\theta \]
